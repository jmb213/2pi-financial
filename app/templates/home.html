Most financial advisory firms implement 'Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT),' which refers to the path-breaking work of Harry Markowitz and others. Their work revolutionized professional investing by carefully defining risk and relating risk to expected returns. Markowitz's analysis highlights the importance of diversification and the need to view the risk of a security in terms of its impact on the portfolio rather than the security's stand-alone risk.

While the ideas underlying MPT are crucial, the theory does not address issues related to investing over many years. Harry Markowitz's analysis did not consider how tomorrow may be different from today. The world may look riskier tomorrow, or less risky, prompting changes in one's portfolio. Importantly, the possibility of changes in the world tomorrow also affects one's portfolio today as one hedges against unfortunate events or prepares to exploit positive developments. Consideration of an evolving world has important ripple effects that influence today's actions.

Since MPT was developed over 50 years ago, there has been much research that challenges and extends the theory. Nobel-prize winning economists have documented that asset returns are more predictable over the long-term than the short-term. The long-run predictability in asset returns has enormous implications for managing wealth over many years.

In addition to changes in expected returns, volatility and correlations vary over time. 2PiFinancial implements not only the ideas in MPT but also more recent research that is relevant for a long-term investing focus. A long-term focus is fundamental to the philosophy of 2PiFinancial. 2PiFinancial's objective is to help you spend, save, and invest so as to get the most enjoyment from your money.

IS BUY AND HOLD ALWAYS THE BEST STRATEGY?

Most experts agree that timing the market, that is, predicting stock returns over the next day, week, or even year, is very difficult. But Eugene Fama and Robert Shiller, Nobel-prize winning economists, agree that there is significant predictability in asset returns over the long term. Their research demonstrates that it is possible to identify times when stock prices are more likely to rise substantially, modestly, or not at all. 2PiFinancial has developed a measure of stock market valuation that is based on the research of Fama, Shiller, and others. The graph uses color to identify times when the S&P 500, a broad index of stock prices, should rise significantly (the market is 'cheap'), rise modestly (the market is 'fair'), and rise little or fall (the market is 'expensive'). Move the cursor over the graph to see how the market performed over various time frames. The average future 10 year real return was over 6.0% when valuation was 'cheap', just under 3% when valuation was 'fair' and slightly negative when valuation was 'expensive.'
